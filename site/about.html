Why this dashboard exists

When residents elect a State government, they hand over tax dollars and trust. In return, the State must deliver on a few bedrock promises: keep us safe and healthy, educate our children, enable good jobs, maintain shared infrastructure, protect us from disasters, manage public money wisely, and continually improve the system so people stay and build their futures here.

Why now

From 2020–24, Hawaiʻi lost tens of thousands of residents to the U.S. mainland. We need a clear, non‑partisan, data‑driven check on where State policy is helping—or missing. This dashboard keeps it light enough to read on your phone, and strong enough to hold leaders accountable.

What we measure

  • Safety: Violent crime per 100,000.
  • Public Health – Outcomes: Years of Potential Life Lost (< 75).
  • Public Health – Access: Uninsured rate.
  • K‑12: 4‑year high‑school graduation rate.
  • Higher Ed: % adults 25+ with a bachelor’s degree.
  • Jobs: Annual unemployment rate.
  • Prosperity & Diversification: Real median household income; tourism share of GDP.
  • Cost of Living: Renter cost burden; unsheltered homelessness per 10k.
  • Infrastructure: % state‑owned bridge deck “poor”; % households with broadband.
  • Energy & Environment: Renewables’ share of electricity generation.
  • Fiscal Stewardship: Rainy‑day fund as % of general‑fund spending.
  • Disaster Resilience: Weather‑disaster losses per capita (CPI‑adjusted).
  • Public Confidence: Voter participation; net domestic migration.

How to read the charts

  • Blue = Hawaiʻi. Gray = simple average of the other U.S. states (DC excluded).
  • Ten‑year trend lines. Every metric is normalized (per‑capita, rate, %), so big and small states compare fairly.
  • If a series has data breaks (e.g., methodology changes), we flag it as non‑comparable.

What we don’t do

  • No opaque composite indices or cherry‑picked time frames.
  • No brag‑sheet rankings—steady progress matters more than “#1”.

Data sources

Authoritative, federal or nationally maintained sources with consistent, 50‑state coverage (e.g., FBI Crime Data Explorer, CDC WISQARS, U.S. Census ACS, BEA, EIA, HUD, NASBO, NOAA NCEI). Each chart’s “Source” link points to the exact dataset/API.
